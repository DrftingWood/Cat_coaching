## 4. Core explanation (0:40 – 2:00)

### Idea 1: A ratio fixes the relationship, not the scale
> VO: When a problem says the ratio of boys to girls is three to two, it is not telling you the count. It is telling you the shape. Three boys for every two girls. The actual numbers could be thirty and twenty, or three hundred and two hundred. The scale is free.

> VO: So give that free scale a name. Call it k. Now boys is three k, girls is two k, and any condition in the problem becomes an equation in one variable.

> ANIMATION: Two buckets labeled "Boys" and "Girls" fill up in a 3:2 shape at multiple scales. Then `FormulaBox` transforming `3:2` into `3k, 2k` with k highlighted in `ACCENT`.

### Idea 2: Product of means = product of extremes
> VO: When two ratios are equal — what we call a proportion — there is a shortcut faster than cross-multiplication. The first and last terms are the extremes; the middle two are the means. Product of means equals product of extremes. Use it whenever you see two ratios on either side of an equals sign.

> ANIMATION: `MathTex(a:b = c:d)` with outer terms glowing `PRIMARY`, inner terms glowing `ACCENT`, then morphing into `a \cdot d = b \cdot c`.

## 5. Worked example (2:00 – 3:00)
- Problem statement: The ratio of Ajay's age to Bina's age is 5:7. Eight years from now, the ratio will be 3:4. Find Bina's present age.
- Step 1: Let ages be 5k and 7k.
- Step 2: Eight years later: (5k + 8) and (7k + 8), ratio 3:4.
- Step 3: Product of means equals product of extremes → 4(5k + 8) = 3(7k + 8).
- Step 4: 20k + 32 = 21k + 24, so k = 8. Bina is 7k = 56.

> VO: Let's see it bite. Ajay and Bina's ages are in the ratio five to seven. Eight years from now, that ratio becomes three to four. Find Bina's age today. Step one, k-method. Ages are five k and seven k. Step two, add eight to both. Step three, product of means equals product of extremes. Step four, solve. k is eight, so Bina is fifty-six.

> ANIMATION: `ProblemBox` with the stem, then `StepReveal` of the four steps synced to VO. `HighlightBox(CORRECT)` on the final answer.

## 6. Common traps (3:00 – 3:15)
> VO: Two traps. One: ratios do not add. Three to four plus two to five is not five to nine. Bring them to a common scale first. Two: a ratio is not a fraction of the whole. In a three to two boy-girl ratio, boys are three-fifths of the room, not three-halves.

> ANIMATION: Two `FormulaBox`es shown with a large red cross-out.
